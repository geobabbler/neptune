## Today's Themes

### 1. Geospatial AI Goes Conversational — and Into Orbit

The geospatial community is watching two distinct but converging AI trajectories today. On the ground, **GeoAlert** launched *Mapflow Agent* in early beta — an LLM-powered chat interface that reads a user's intent, builds a geospatial processing pipeline on the fly, and runs it on Mapflow's engine. The impetus is telling: more than half of all Mapflow projects were being created but never processed, a "project activation rate" problem that a natural-language interface is designed to fix. It represents one of the more substantive deployments of conversational AI into an operational geospatial workflow to date.

In orbit, Pixxel's upcoming *Pathfinder* satellite will take AI one step further, running full-stack language models and inference directly on-board — making it one of the first Earth observation platforms to attempt genuine on-orbit AI processing rather than simply downlinking raw imagery for analysis on the ground. Together, these two stories mark a Friday in which "AI-powered geospatial" moved visibly from marketing language toward shipped products.

### 2. Satellites, Constellations, and the Race to Measure the Planet Daily

EarthDaily Analytics confirmed initial contact with six newly launched satellites — EDC-02 through EDC-07 — carried to orbit aboard a SpaceX Falcon 9 rideshare. The EarthDaily Constellation is designed around the ambitious goal of daily, global, calibrated measurement of planetary change, and today's confirmation marks a meaningful milestone. At the research end of the spectrum, a piece from *Remote Sensing on Medium* describes a project that used satellite imagery combined with deep learning to map forest canopy height across the entire Basque Country — an example of how dense open-source satellite archives, paired with modern ML, are enabling regional environmental baselines that would have been impractical even five years ago.

A researcher spotlight from *Earth Observation News* (University of Würzburg) profiles Sebastian Buchelt, whose work on snowmelt dynamics in high-alpine environments blends time-series satellite analysis with in-situ timelapse cameras — a useful reminder that the most actionable EO science often fuses spaceborne and ground-truth data rather than relying on either alone.

### 3. Disaster Mapping at Multiple Scales — Floods, Tsunamis, and Twisters

Hazard mapping is a recurring undercurrent across today's feeds. Two independent pieces on GIS-based flood risk mapping — one framed as a coursework exercise, another as a broader introduction to satellite-derived flood monitoring — reflect the growing accessibility of these methods. The practical toolchain (Sentinel SAR, Google Earth Engine, QGIS) is now sufficiently documented that student-level projects can reproduce meaningful flood extent maps, a sign of genuine democratization.

The higher-stakes end of the hazard spectrum surfaces via a recently published *Science* paper on a 481-metre tsunami runup in a cruise-ship-frequented Alaskan fjord — the second-largest tsunami runup ever recorded. The mechanism is directly tied to climate change: decades of glacier retreat meant the triggering landslide fell into open water rather than onto ice, generating a wave that would not have occurred under historical conditions. On the tornado front, *UBIQUE (AGS)* published a map-of-the-week showing Illinois recorded 119 tornado reports through April — nearly double its historical average and more than double any other state this year — a striking spatial anomaly in an already active spring severe weather season.
